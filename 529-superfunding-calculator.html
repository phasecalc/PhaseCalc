How this 529 superfunding calculator works

"Superfunding" a 529 plan means front-loading up to five years of gifting into one lump-sum contribution, then using a special IRS election to avoid triggering gift tax or eating into your lifetime exemption. This calculator takes your lump sum, splits it evenly across the number of contributors you enter (typically two parents, sometimes grandparents too), and — if you elect 5-year averaging — divides each contributor's share by five to find the amount reported to the IRS each year. It then compares that per-year, per-contributor figure against the annual gift tax exclusion you enter (editable, since it's indexed for inflation and changes most years) to show exactly how much fits inside the exclusion and how much, if any, spills into your lifetime gift and estate tax exemption. Separately, it projects the lump sum forward at your expected return to show how much of the ending balance is tax-free investment growth on top of your original contribution. The 5-year election, explained

Under IRC section 529(c)(2)(B), a contribution to a 529 plan is treated as a gift to the beneficiary in the year it's made. Ordinarily, any gift above the annual exclusion counts immediately against your lifetime gift and estate tax exemption. The 5-year election changes the timing: it lets you treat one lump-sum 529 contribution as if you'd made it in five equal installments over five calendar years, purely for gift-tax accounting. Nothing about the actual 529 account changes — the full amount is invested and growing from day one — only the IRS paperwork spreads it out.

To make the election, you (or each contributing individual) file IRS Form 709 for the year of the contribution, check the box electing to split the gift over five years, and report one-fifth of the gift in that return. You generally don't need to file Form 709 again in years two through five unless you make other reportable gifts to the same person. Because each contributor's exclusion is separate, two parents contributing together effectively double the tax-free superfunding capacity, and grandparents or other relatives can layer their own five-year elections on top — as long as no one makes additional gifts to the same beneficiary during the five-year window, which would eat into that year's remaining exclusion.

Gift tax exclusion basics

The annual gift tax exclusion is the amount one person can give another person in a calendar year without filing a gift tax return or using any of their lifetime exemption. It's $19,000 per recipient for 2026, indexed for inflation in $1,000 increments, which is why this calculator makes the exclusion an editable field rather than a hard-coded constant — plug in whatever the current year's figure is, or model a future year's expected increase. The exclusion applies per giver, per recipient: a married couple can together give a child $38,000 a year (via gift-splitting) without any reporting at all, and that same child's grandparents have their own separate exclusion again.

Superfunding simply multiplies that annual number by five. At the 2026 exclusion of $19,000, one contributor can front-load $95,000; two contributors (say, both parents) can front-load $190,000 — all reportable via Form 709 but requiring zero use of the far larger lifetime exemption ($15 million per person in 2026). Contribute more than that five-year cap and the excess isn't taxed outright, but it does count against your lifetime exemption — which is why the schedule table below flags any amount that spills over.

Tax-free qualified withdrawal rules

The entire point of a 529 plan is that investment growth is never taxed at the federal level — not while it compounds, and not when it comes out — as long as withdrawals pay for qualified education expenses. That covers tuition and mandatory fees, room and board (up to the school's cost-of-attendance figure), books and supplies, required equipment and computers, up to $10,000 per year of K-12 tuition, registered apprenticeship program costs, and up to $10,000 lifetime toward repaying student loans (for the beneficiary or a sibling). Most states offer the same tax-free treatment on withdrawals, and many also give an upfront state income tax deduction or credit for contributions.

That's what the amber-vs-blue chart above visualizes: the amber line is your principal (already after-tax money, never taxed again regardless of use), and the shaded gap up to the blue line is investment growth that stays completely untaxed if spent on qualified expenses. Withdraw that growth for something non-qualified instead, and only the earnings portion is taxed as ordinary income plus a 10% penalty — your original contribution always comes out free and clear, and the penalty itself is waived for scholarships, the beneficiary's death or disability, and a few other exceptions.

The 529-to-Roth IRA rollover option

If a beneficiary doesn't end up needing all the money — a full scholarship, a shorter degree, or a change of plans — a provision added by SECURE 2.0 allows rolling up to $35,000 lifetime, per beneficiary, from a 529 plan directly into a Roth IRA in that beneficiary's name, with no income tax or penalty. The rollover is still capped by that year's ordinary Roth IRA contribution limit, the 529 account must have been open for at least 15 years, and contributions (and their earnings) made within the last five years aren't eligible. It's a narrow but genuinely useful backstop: money that might otherwise face tax and penalty on a non-qualified withdrawal can instead become tax-free retirement savings for the beneficiary.

A few practical notes: contributions above the 5-year cap aren't a mistake, just a trade-off — the lifetime exemption is large enough ($15 million per person in 2026) that most families never come close to owing actual gift tax, so going over is mainly a paperwork and estate-planning consideration, not a tax bill today. Dying partway through the five-year window pulls the unelapsed years back into your taxable estate. And 529 contribution limits (the total a single account can ever hold, often $400,000-$550,000+ depending on the state) are a separate ceiling from the gift-tax exclusion modeled here — check your plan's aggregate limit before superfunding into an account that's already substantial.

Frequently asked questions

What is 529 plan superfunding?

Contributing up to five years' worth of the annual gift tax exclusion to a 529 plan in one lump sum, then electing to spread it over five calendar years for gift-tax purposes — up to $95,000 per contributor in 2026, or $190,000 for two.

How does the 5-year gift-tax averaging election work?

You file Form 709 in the contribution year, elect to treat the gift as made in five equal annual installments, and each installment can then fit inside that year's annual exclusion instead of immediately using your lifetime exemption.

What is the annual gift tax exclusion, and how does it apply here?

It's the amount ($19,000 in 2026, indexed yearly) one person can gift another without filing a return or touching their lifetime exemption. Each 529 contributor's gifts to the beneficiary are measured against their own exclusion.

How much can I superfund into a 529 plan in 2026?

$95,000 per contributor ($19,000 x 5), or $190,000 for a couple contributing jointly. Grandparents and others each have their own separate $95,000 capacity for the same beneficiary.

Do I have to pay gift tax if I superfund?

Almost never. Amounts above the exclusion reduce your lifetime exemption ($15 million per person in 2026) rather than triggering a tax bill — you'd only owe tax if cumulative lifetime gifts exceeded that exemption.

What if I make other gifts to the same beneficiary during the 5 years?

They reduce the exclusion still available that year, which can push part of the superfunded gift into using your lifetime exemption. Superfunding works cleanest as the only gift to that beneficiary for five years.

Are 529 withdrawals really tax-free?

Yes, federally, for qualified education expenses — tuition, room and board, books, up to $10,000/year of K-12 tuition, and up to $10,000 lifetime of student loan repayment. Non-qualified withdrawals owe ordinary income tax plus a 10% penalty on earnings only.

What if my child doesn't go to college?

Change the beneficiary to another qualifying family member penalty-free, leave it invested for later, or withdraw it and pay tax plus a 10% penalty on just the earnings (waived for scholarships, death, or disability).

Can I roll over unused 529 funds to a Roth IRA?

Yes — up to $35,000 lifetime per beneficiary, subject to annual Roth contribution limits, if the account has been open 15+ years and the funds aren't from the last five years of contributions or earnings.

Do I need to file Form 709 if I superfund?

Yes, in the year of the contribution, to make the 5-year election — even if the entire amount fits within the annual exclusion and no tax is owed.

What happens if I die during the 5-year election period?

The portions allocated to the remaining years are pulled back into your taxable estate rather than continuing to be treated as already-made gifts.

Can grandparents also superfund a 529 plan?

Yes — each contributing individual has their own separate five-year exclusion capacity for the same beneficiary, as long as no one adds other gifts to that beneficiary during the window.
