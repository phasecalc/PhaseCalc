What this calculator models

This is the classic high-earner real estate play, drawn end to end. One spouse earns a large W-2 — say $300,000. The other spouse has no full-time job and qualifies as a real estate professional under §469(c)(7). The couple buys a rental, orders a cost segregation study, and 100% bonus depreciation converts a quarter of the building into a first-year deduction. On paper the rental loses six figures; in cash it roughly breaks even. Because the REP spouse materially participates, that paper loss is non-passive — and on a joint return it offsets the W-2, producing a tax refund that would make the property's actual cash flow blush.

The calculator runs the whole arc: the year-1 loss waterfall and W-2 offset, every year of the hold (watching for the year the paper losses dry up), the sale with selling costs, depreciation recapture, and capital gains tax — and then puts the result head-to-head against simply putting the same cash in an index fund, with the rental path reinvesting its tax savings and cash flow at the same index return so the comparison is honest. What it does not do is tell you whether you qualify for REPS. Nothing on this page is tax advice; it's a scenario planner for a conversation with a CPA.

What real estate professional status actually is

REPS is a tax classification, not a license — no exam, no certificate, just facts and hours. To qualify for a given year, a taxpayer must pass three tests:

1. The more-than-half test. More than 50% of all personal-service working hours must be in real property trades or businesses in which they materially participate — development, construction, acquisition, rental, management, brokerage. This is the test that disqualifies almost everyone with a full-time job: 2,000 hours at an employer means needing 2,001 in real estate.

2. The 750-hour test. More than 750 hours in those real property activities during the year — about 14.5 hours a week. Hours must be the taxpayer's own; spouses cannot combine hours for this test or the first one.

3. Material participation in the rental itself. Qualifying as a REP only removes the automatic passive label. The taxpayer must also materially participate in the specific rental — most commonly via the 500-hour or "substantially all" tests — or make a grouping election to treat all rental interests as one activity. Miss this step and the losses stay passive anyway.

All three tests are about documentation as much as hours. The Tax Court has sided with taxpayers who kept contemporaneous logs — dated entries, task descriptions, hours — and against those who reconstructed estimates after an audit notice arrived. If you're pursuing this, keep the log as you go (a REPS hours log template makes it painless) and be honest about whether the hours are real. "On call" time and investor-level activities like reviewing statements generally don't count.

Why this is a married-filing-jointly strategy

The elegant asymmetry at the heart of the strategy: the hour tests are applied per spouse, but the tax benefit lands on the joint return. A stay-at-home spouse who spends 800+ hours a year genuinely managing the rental — and has no other job competing for the more-than-half test — can qualify alone. Once their rental losses are non-passive, the joint return nets them against the household's combined income, including the other spouse's W-2. File separately and the mechanism breaks: the losses stay on the REP spouse's return with little income to offset. That's why the calculator fixes filing status to MFJ.

The baseline: what happens without REPS

Rental real estate is per se passive under §469, no matter how many hours you work — unless REPS (or short-term rental treatment) applies. Passive losses can only offset passive income. There's a consolation prize for moderate earners: up to $25,000 of rental losses can offset ordinary income with active participation, but it phases out at 50 cents per dollar of modified AGI above $100,000 and is gone entirely at $150,000. For the $300k household this tool is built around, the no-REPS baseline is stark: $0 usable today. The losses aren't destroyed — they're suspended, carried forward against future passive income, and released in full when the property sells — but a deduction claimed today at 32%+ beats the same deduction released a decade from now, both on time value and on rate arbitrage. The hero number in the results strip shows this gap directly.

Cost segregation + 100% bonus: manufacturing the year-1 loss

Straight-line depreciation spreads the building's basis over 27.5 years — useful, but slow. A cost segregation study is an engineering analysis that identifies the components of the property that aren't really "building": carpet, appliances, cabinetry (5-year), certain fixtures (7-year), driveways, fencing, landscaping (15-year). For residential rentals, 20–35% of the building basis typically reclassifies; the calculator defaults to 25%.

The multiplier is bonus depreciation. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 2025) restored 100% bonus permanently for qualifying property acquired after January 19, 2025 — no more phase-down anxiety. Everything the study reclassifies into 5/7/15-year property is deductible in full, in year one. On a $500,000 purchase with 20% land, that's a $400,000 building basis, $100,000 of which becomes a first-year deduction — stacked on top of straight-line depreciation on the remainder, mortgage interest, and operating costs. The result is the waterfall above: a property that roughly breaks even in cash shows a six-figure loss on paper.

Year-1 deduction (cost seg) = building basis × reclass % × 100% bonus
+ (building basis − reclassified) ÷ 27.5
+ mortgage interest + operating costs + study fee

Flat-fee virtual studies for single-family properties run $3,000–$5,000 — trivially worth it when the incremental deduction is worth $30,000+ in taxes, and worth skipping when it isn't (toggle the method above and watch the gap). One honesty note: bonus depreciation doesn't create deductions, it accelerates them. You're trading smaller deductions in years 2–27 and a bigger recapture bill at sale for a large deduction now. That trade is usually favorable at high marginal rates — but it's a trade.

Your marginal rate: 2026 federal brackets (MFJ)

The calculator multiplies the paper loss by your combined federal + state marginal rate — the rate on your last dollar, not your average rate. For 2026 (per Rev. Proc. 2025-32, filing in early 2027), the MFJ brackets on taxable income (after the $32,200 standard deduction) are:

Two wrinkles worth knowing: a very large loss can straddle brackets, so the true savings are slightly less than loss × top rate (the calculator uses the flat marginal rate you enter — a fine planning approximation); and every dollar of deduction is worth more at 37% than at 22%, which is why this strategy is a high-earner strategy. There's also a hidden bonus not modeled here: reducing AGI can restore phased-out benefits and reduce the 3.8% NIIT base.

The exit bill: depreciation recapture

Every dollar of depreciation you deduct reduces your cost basis, and the IRS collects on the way out. At sale, gain attributable to prior straight-line depreciation on the building is unrecaptured §1250 gain, taxed at up to 25% — notably higher than the 15% LTCG rate most sellers pay on the rest. The cost-segregated 5/7/15-year property is technically §1245 property, recaptured at ordinary rates — this calculator simplifies by taxing all recapture at 25%, which modestly flatters the cost-seg scenario for high-bracket sellers; a CPA will run the exact split. Gain beyond total depreciation is regular long-term capital gain at your entered rate (15%, or 20% + 3.8% NIIT at high incomes — the capital gains calculator handles that bracket math).

Even with recapture, the deal often works: you deducted at a 32–37% combined marginal rate and repay at 25%, years later, in inflated dollars — rate arbitrage plus an interest-free loan from Treasury. And the bill is avoidable: a 1031 exchange rolls the gain and recapture into a replacement property indefinitely (not modeled here — it's a rabbit hole of deadlines and intermediaries), and heirs receive a stepped-up basis that erases the deferred tax entirely. "Defer, exchange, die" is a real strategy, morbid as it reads.

The §461(l) ceiling

One more guardrail: even with REPS, the excess business loss limitation caps how much net business loss can offset non-business income (like wages) in a single year — $512,000 for MFJ in 2026 (OBBBA re-based the thresholds; they index annually). A single-rental cost seg rarely gets close, but stack bonus depreciation on a large or multiple properties and you can hit the ceiling; the excess carries forward as a net operating loss rather than vanishing. The calculator flags your scenario if the year-1 loss crosses it.

Audit risk, honestly

REPS claims by households with large W-2s are a recognized IRS audit target, and the case law is a graveyard of taxpayers with round-number hour estimates and no records. The pattern in the losses is consistent: hours reconstructed after the fact, "investor" activities counted as participation, travel and education hours padding the log, and claimed hours that physically don't fit alongside a full-time job. The pattern in the wins is equally consistent: contemporaneous, credible, specific logs. If the qualifying spouse genuinely runs the property — leasing, maintenance coordination, bookkeeping, renovations — the hours are real and documentable. Keep the log from day one (template here), don't round to suspiciously tidy numbers, and treat the 750 hours as a floor you clear with room to spare, not a target to engineer.

When this strategy does not make sense

Low marginal rate. At a 22–24% combined rate, the year-1 offset shrinks and the recapture rate (25%) can exceed your deduction rate — the arbitrage inverts. This is a strategy that scales with your bracket.

Bad property economics. Tax savings are a percentage rescue on a loss that's real if the deal is bad. A property bleeding $1,000/month of true cash flow doesn't become good because April improved; run it through the rental property calculator on its own merits first. The wealth chart above makes this vivid — if the bars can't catch the index line even with the tax savings stacked on top, the market is telling you something.

The hours aren't real. If the qualifying spouse doesn't actually want to spend 750+ hours a year on real estate, the strategy fails either honestly (don't claim it) or expensively (claim it and lose the audit). A fabricated log is not a tax strategy.

Short expected hold with no 1031 appetite. Sell in year 2–3 and recapture claws back most of the acceleration you just paid a study fee to create.

Frequently asked questions

Can rental losses offset W-2 income?

Normally no — they're passive, and above $150k MAGI they're fully suspended. With REPS and material participation, they become non-passive and offset any income on the joint return, including a spouse's W-2.

What is real estate professional status?

A §469(c)(7) classification requiring more than half of your working hours and 750+ hours per year in real property trades you materially participate in — plus material participation in the rental itself. No license involved; it's re-tested every year.

How many hours do you need for REPS?

750+ hours a year in real property activities, and more than half of your total working time — which is why it's nearly impossible alongside a full-time job, and why the one-spouse structure exists. Document with contemporaneous logs.

Does cost segregation work for one rental?

Usually — flat-fee virtual studies ($3,000–$5,000) typically reclassify 20–35% of building basis, all deductible in year 1 under permanent 100% bonus depreciation. The deduction only offsets W-2 income if REPS (or STR treatment) applies.

What is depreciation recapture?

At sale, gain attributable to prior depreciation is taxed at up to 25% (straight-line §1250) or ordinary rates (cost-seg'd §1245 property). It makes depreciation a deferral + rate-arbitrage play, not free money — unless you 1031 or hold until step-up.

Do both spouses need to qualify?

No — one spouse qualifies using their own hours alone, and the joint return spreads the benefit across both incomes. Filing separately breaks the strategy.

What happens to the losses without REPS?

Up to $25k offsets other income below $100k MAGI, phasing to zero at $150k. Above that, losses suspend and carry forward — released against future passive income or at sale. Usable today at a $300k W-2: $0.

Is bonus depreciation still 100%?

Yes — the OBBBA (July 2025) made 100% bonus permanent for qualifying property acquired after January 19, 2025, ending the scheduled phase-down.
